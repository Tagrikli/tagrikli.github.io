Vision-Based Edge Occupancy Detection

04/01/2026

This graduation project focused on replacing expensive laser scanners in automated parking systems with a computer vision–based solution. The goal was to detect whether a vehicle extends beyond pallet edges—an error that can cause serious collisions during automated transport—using low-cost cameras and edge AI hardware.

Existing industrial systems rely on laser sensors costing thousands of euros per unit and suffer from reliability issues with dark vehicle surfaces. My objective was to demonstrate that a carefully engineered deep learning system could match their accuracy at a fraction of the cost.

The Challenge

The main technical challenge was building a reliable safety-critical classifier from limited real-world data collected in a single parking facility. While occupied pallets naturally vary with different vehicles, empty pallets are visually uniform, creating strong risks of overfitting and poor generalization.

Additional constraints included real-time inference requirements, low hardware budgets, and strict limits on false negatives, where missed detections could lead to physical damage.

The Solution

Data Pipeline: I designed a semi-automated labeling system that reduced manual annotation effort from over 200 hours to roughly 2 hours, enabling efficient creation of a high-quality dataset.

Model Architecture: A MobileNetV3-based convolutional neural network was implemented using transfer learning, optimized for deployment on embedded devices such as Raspberry Pi and Jetson platforms.

Validation Methodology: Through systematic error analysis, I refined the augmentation and testing pipeline after discovering that common occlusion techniques introduced misleading artifacts. This led to a more realistic and robust evaluation framework.

Synthetic Data Generation: To address limited environmental diversity, I developed a Blender-based rendering pipeline that generated thousands of labeled synthetic images. Pre-training on this dataset significantly improved generalization to unseen conditions.

Deployment Architecture: A modular real-time processing pipeline was designed for multi-camera monitoring, enabling low-latency inference and redundancy-based safety guarantees.

Results

The final system achieved up to 98.94% validation accuracy on real operational data and maintained strong performance under simulated environmental variations. Synthetic pre-training further improved robustness while reducing false positives by over 70%.

Compared to traditional laser-based systems, the solution offers approximately 96% cost reduction while eliminating color-dependent failure modes.

Beyond technical performance, this project provided hands-on experience in building industrial-grade machine learning systems under real operational constraints—from data acquisition and validation design to deployment planning and ethical considerations.
